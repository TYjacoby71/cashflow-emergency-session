Play 1 of 5

Deploy AI Agents to Handle Work

Replace repetitive human tasks with AI agents configured for your specific workflows. Target: 10–40 hours/week recovered per agent deployed, equivalent to $2,000–$8,000/month in labor cost.

Best for
  • Founders whose team spends significant time on manual, repetitive tasks
  • Agencies or operators with repeatable client deliverables (reports, outreach, triage)
  • Anyone paying for human work that follows a predictable pattern
The mechanics

An AI agent is not a chatbot. It’s a configured system with a persona, a set of skills, and a defined task scope. The key is identifying a specific, bounded workflow — not deploying AI broadly and hoping something sticks.

What agents can handle today:

  • Inbound lead triage and qualification (route to human only when ready)
  • Client onboarding data collection and document prep
  • Weekly reporting and status updates from your tools
  • Outreach follow-up sequences (email, Slack, CRM)
  • Support ticket triage, FAQ resolution, and escalation routing
Launch checklist
  • Identify 1–3 workflows your team does that follow a consistent pattern (write it out in 5 steps)
  • Measure the current time cost: how many hours/week, at what hourly equivalent?
  • Define the agent’s scope: what it handles, what triggers escalation to a human
  • Configure the agent with the right persona, tools, and task checklist
  • Run 3–5 test scenarios before going live — edge cases are where agents break
  • Set a measurement baseline: response time, error rate, tasks completed per week
  • Review agent output weekly for the first month; refine the configuration

Implementation Guide

Step 1 — Map the workflow
WORKFLOW MAPPING TEMPLATEWorkflow name: [e.g., "Inbound lead qualification"] Trigger: [What starts the workflow? e.g., "New form fill from website"] Steps (in order): 1. [First thing that happens] 2. [Second thing] 3. [Third thing — if/else branches here?] 4. [What output is produced?] 5. [Who receives the output?] Time cost: [X] hours/week at [Y] hourly rate = $[Z]/week Frequency: [Daily / Weekly / Per customer / etc.] Human judgment required: [Yes/No — if yes, where in the workflow?] Error consequences if agent gets it wrong: [Low / Medium / High]
Step 2 — Define the agent configuration

Every agent needs three things before it can work: a persona (who it is, what it knows), a skill set (what tools and actions it has access to), and a checklist (the specific steps it follows for each task).

  • Persona: Give the agent a role title and context. "You are a lead qualification specialist for [Company]. Your job is to assess inbound leads and route them appropriately."
  • Skills: What tools can it read from / write to? CRM? Email? Slack? Define these explicitly.
  • Checklist: The step-by-step procedure it follows. If the workflow map in Step 1 is good, the checklist writes itself.
Step 3 — Test before launch
TEST SCENARIO CHECKLISTBefore deploying any agent, run these 5 tests: Test 1: Normal case — does it handle the standard workflow correctly? Test 2: Edge case A — what happens with incomplete input? Test 3: Edge case B — what happens with ambiguous input? Test 4: Escalation trigger — does it correctly route to a human when needed? Test 5: Output format — is the output usable by whoever receives it? Pass rate required before go-live: 5/5 on escalation, 4/5 on normal/edge cases.
The compound effect: One agent handling lead triage saves 15 hours/week. That’s 60 hours/month your team reclaims for higher-leverage work. At a $50/hour equivalent, that’s $3,000/month in recovered capacity — from one configuration.

Audit Your Operations for $50K+ in Waste

Play 3 of 5

Audit Your Operations for $50K+ in Waste

Find and eliminate the manual process waste most $500K–$5M businesses carry without knowing it. Target: $50,000+ in annual cost identified per audit. Immediate reduction visible within 30 days.

Best for
  • Founders who feel like the business is working hard but not getting faster
  • Companies with 5–50 employees and growing operational complexity
  • Anyone whose team keeps saying “we need more people” before processes have been optimized
The mechanics

Every $500K–$5M business carries operational drag. It shows up as: tasks that require a human to forward an email, reports assembled manually from tools that could talk directly, approval chains that delay work by 24–72 hours. None of this feels like waste because it’s “just how it works.” An AI-powered audit maps it and prices the drag.

Where the waste hides:

  • Data handoffs: Anything that gets copy-pasted between tools (CRM → spreadsheet → report)
  • Approval delays: Decisions that wait in someone’s inbox instead of being automated
  • Status update overhead: Time spent answering “where is this?” instead of doing the work
  • Duplicate data entry: The same information entered into two or more systems
  • Manual scheduling: Calendar coordination done by humans instead of automated booking tools
Launch checklist
  • List every recurring task your team does weekly — everything, including internal meetings
  • For each task: how long does it take, how often, could AI or automation handle it?
  • Identify the top 5 tasks by time-cost (hours/week × hourly rate)
  • Map the data flow: where does information enter your business and where does it go?
  • Find every manual handoff point — these are your automation opportunities
  • Calculate the annual cost of each manual process (time × hourly rate × 52 weeks)
  • Prioritize by: (1) highest cost, (2) easiest to automate, (3) lowest risk if AI gets it slightly wrong

Audit Framework

The 4-category scan
OPERATIONAL WASTE SCANCategory 1: COMMUNICATION OVERHEAD - How many hours/week does your team spend on internal status updates? - How many meetings could be replaced by a shared dashboard? - How many emails are "just checking in" on work that has no tracking system? Category 2: DATA MOVEMENT - How many times does the same data get re-entered into different tools? - What reports are assembled manually from tools that have APIs? - What would break if one person went on vacation for 2 weeks? Category 3: APPROVAL & DECISION DELAYS - What decisions require a senior person's sign-off that could be pre-approved by rule? - How long does the average approval take? What's the delay cost in revenue or speed? - Which approvals could be automated with a clear decision tree? Category 4: CUSTOMER-FACING FRICTION - How long does it take a new client to get onboarded from signed contract to active? - How many steps in your sales or fulfillment process require a human to move forward? - What do clients ask repeatedly that should be answered automatically?
What to do with the audit findings
  1. Tier 1 — Automate immediately: Tasks with $5,000+/year cost and clear automation path
  2. Tier 2 — Automate in 30 days: Tasks with $1,000–$5,000/year cost and moderate complexity
  3. Tier 3 — Eliminate: Tasks you do because you always have, not because they create value
  4. Tier 4 — Delegate to AI agent: Tasks with high frequency but low judgment requirement (see Play 1)
The math on one finding: A 3-person ops team spending 30 minutes per day on manual reporting = 7.5 hours/week at $40/hour = $15,600/year. One integration (Play 4) eliminates it permanently. The ROI on a $500 audit that finds this is 31x in year one.

Connect AI to Your Existing Stack

Play 4 of 5

Connect AI to Your Existing Stack

Bridge your disconnected tools with AI-powered integrations. Stop copying data between systems and start letting your stack work as one coordinated machine. Target: 10–30 hours/week of manual data movement eliminated.

Best for
  • Founders running 5+ SaaS tools that don’t natively talk to each other
  • Teams where CRM, email, project management, and billing are all in separate systems
  • Anyone who has tried Zapier but hit limitations on complex, multi-step workflows
The mechanics

Modern businesses run on 15–30 SaaS tools on average. Those tools were not designed to work together. The result: humans become the connective tissue — manually moving data, translating formats, and triggering actions that should happen automatically. AI integration fixes this at the workflow level, not just the data level.

The integration hierarchy (prioritize in this order):

  • Level 1 — Native integrations: If Tool A and Tool B have a built-in connection, use it first. Zero cost, zero maintenance.
  • Level 2 — API integrations: When native isn’t available. Requires a developer or Integration Wizard configuration. One-time setup, perpetual value.
  • Level 3 — AI middleware: When data needs transformation or judgment before it moves. An AI layer reads, interprets, and routes between systems.
  • Level 4 — Agent-driven workflows: Full AI orchestration. An agent monitors inputs, triggers actions, and handles exceptions. (See Play 1.)
Launch checklist
  • List every tool your business runs (SaaS, cloud, internal apps) — even the legacy ones
  • Draw a data flow map: what information needs to move from where to where, and how often?
  • Identify the 3 most painful data handoffs (where humans are the connective tissue)
  • Check for native integrations first on all 3 — most founders don’t know these exist
  • For gaps: determine if Level 2 (API) or Level 3 (AI middleware) is the right solution
  • Configure and test the first integration before moving to the second
  • Measure before and after: hours saved per week, error rate reduction

Integration Patterns That Work

High-impact integration patterns
INTEGRATION PATTERN LIBRARYPattern 1: CRM → Project Management When a deal closes in your CRM, automatically create a project in your PM tool with the client name, contract value, and start date pre-filled. Tools: Salesforce/HubSpot → Asana/Linear/Notion Value: Eliminates 15–30 minutes of manual handoff per client. Pattern 2: Form Fill → CRM → Email Sequence When a form is submitted, create a CRM contact, score the lead, and trigger a personalized email sequence — no human in the loop. Tools: Typeform/Webflow → HubSpot/Close → Mailchimp/ActiveCampaign Value: Sub-5-minute lead response time, 24/7, zero labor cost. Pattern 3: Billing → Reporting → Slack When an invoice is paid, update your revenue dashboard and post a real-time notification to your finance Slack channel. Tools: Stripe → Notion/Google Sheets → Slack Value: Real-time revenue visibility without manual reporting. Pattern 4: Support Ticket → Knowledge Base → Resolution When a ticket arrives, check the knowledge base for a matching answer. If confidence is high, auto-respond. If low, route to human with context. Tools: Intercom/Zendesk → Notion/Confluence → AI layer Value: 40–70% of support tickets resolved without human intervention.
Common integration mistakes
  • Integrating before cleaning data: Bad data in, bad data out. Deduplicate and standardize your CRM before connecting it to anything.
  • Over-automating too fast: Test every integration manually for one week before turning off human oversight.
  • No error alerting: Every integration needs a failure notification. Silent failures are the worst kind.
  • Integrating what should be eliminated: Some workflows shouldn’t be automated — they should be killed. Run Play 3 first if you’re not sure.
The velocity multiplier: A team with a fully integrated stack moves 2–3x faster than one without. Not because they work harder — because they stop doing the same thing twice. Integration is the highest-leverage operational investment most businesses haven’t made yet.

Build an AI-Powered Revenue System

Chain Plays 1–4 into a self-reinforcing revenue system where AI handles the pipeline, operations, and customer experience end-to-end. This is the compounding play. Target: measurable revenue increase within 90 days of full deployment.

Best for
  • Founders who have already implemented at least one of Plays 1–4
  • Businesses at $500K–$5M revenue with ambition to scale without proportional headcount growth
  • Operators who want to compete at a level that previously required 3× their current team size
The mechanics

Individual AI plays give you efficiency gains. An AI revenue system gives you compounding leverage. When your agent handles inbound (Play 1), your tools are integrated (Play 4), your operations are lean (Play 3), and your infrastructure is funded by credits (Play 2), the entire business runs faster — at lower cost — with better data.

The four-layer AI revenue architecture:

  • Layer 1 — Acquisition: AI handles lead capture, qualification, and initial outreach. Humans focus only on relationships and closing.
  • Layer 2 — Operations: AI handles status updates, reporting, onboarding steps, and data movement. Humans focus on decisions and exceptions.
  • Layer 3 — Retention: AI handles check-ins, usage monitoring, and renewal signals. Humans focus on relationships with accounts that need attention.
  • Layer 4 — Intelligence: All layers feed data into a single revenue dashboard. You can see where leads stall, where clients churn, and where waste is highest — in real time.
Launch checklist
  • Confirm which of Plays 1–4 are already live and working in your business
  • Map the gaps: where does AI hand off to a human? Is that handoff necessary?
  • Identify the single highest-leverage automation not yet implemented
  • Design the intelligence layer: what metrics does the business need to see daily/weekly?
  • Build a revenue dashboard that pulls from your integrated stack (no manual updates)
  • Run a 30-day baseline: track revenue, cost per acquisition, and team time allocation
  • At day 30: identify the next bottleneck and assign it to the next play iteration

Chapter 7

72-Hour AI Launch Sprint

This is your execution calendar. Run it in parallel with whichever play you selected. The sprint assumes you’ve committed to one play and are starting at 8am on Day 1. The goal: your first AI system is live — tested, deployed, and producing output — by the end of Day 3.

Go / No-Go rule: At the end of each day, ask: “Did I complete the configuration work I planned? Did I run the required tests?” If No on either — the bottleneck is execution speed, not the play. Get someone to help or reduce scope. Ship something smaller that works.
Day 1 Audit & Select
  • 8:00–9:00 YOU Complete the AI Play Selector (Chapter 1). Commit to one play. Write it down. Tell your team. The mistake most founders make is staying in evaluation mode for two weeks instead of picking and executing.
  • 9:00–11:00 YOU Read your play chapter in full. Complete the full launch checklist on paper before touching any tool. Know what you’re building before you build it.
  • 11:00–12:00 YOU Map the specific workflow you’re targeting. Use the mapping template from your play chapter. If you can’t describe the workflow in 5 steps, you don’t know it well enough to automate it yet.
  • 12:00–14:00 YOU Gather access credentials for every tool involved in the workflow: login to the platforms, verify API access if needed, confirm you have permission to integrate or configure. Blocked access on Day 2 kills sprints.
  • 14:00–17:00 YOU Begin configuration. For Play 1: write the agent persona and checklist. For Play 2: submit the first two credit applications. For Play 3: run the 4-category waste scan. For Play 4: identify and test the first native integration.
  • 17:00–18:00 YOU Day 1 debrief: what did you configure? What is blocked? What needs to be done before Day 2 starts? Write tomorrow’s first 3 tasks tonight — don’t improvise the morning.
Day 2 Configure & Test
  • 8:00–9:00 YOU Review Day 1 configuration. Is it complete enough to test? If not: what is the minimum viable version you can test today? Reduce scope if needed — a working smaller system beats a broken full system.
  • 9:00–12:00 YOU Complete configuration for Day 2 target. For Play 1: finish agent skill setup and prompt tuning. For Play 2: submit remaining tier-1 credit applications. For Play 3: price every finding and build the tier list. For Play 4: configure the first API integration end-to-end.
  • 12:00–14:00 YOU Run test scenarios. Use the test checklist from your play chapter. Document every failure. Do not fix failures during the test run — log them and fix after the full test is complete.
  • 14:00–15:00 YOU ⚑ GO / NO-GO CHECK: Did you complete at least 70% of your play’s launch checklist? Did you run at least 3 of the 5 test scenarios? If not — diagnose now. What took longer than expected? What do you need to cut to ship by Day 3?
  • 15:00–17:00 YOU Fix the test failures identified at midday. Re-run failed tests. Document what changed. Set a pass/fail threshold for Day 3 go-live: what must work for this to be worth deploying?
  • 17:00–18:00 YOU Day 2 debrief: Update your launch checklist. Log what’s done, what’s in progress, what’s cut. Write tomorrow’s deployment plan: what goes live, who needs to be notified, how will you measure success on Day 3.
Day 3 Launch & Measure
  • 8:00–9:00 YOU Final pre-launch check: are all tests passing at your minimum threshold? Are all access credentials live? Is the failure notification set up? Do not launch without error alerting — silent failures are worse than no deployment.
  • 9:00–11:00 YOU Deploy. For Play 1: activate the agent on the first live workflow. For Play 2: confirm credit applications submitted and set follow-up reminders. For Play 3: present audit findings to the team. For Play 4: flip the integration live and monitor the first 10 data passes.
  • 11:00–14:00 YOU Monitor live. Keep an eye on the first outputs from your deployed system. Respond to any errors or unexpected behavior immediately. This is not the time to start configuring the next thing — watch what you just launched.
  • 14:00–16:00 YOU Measure Day 3 results against baseline. How many tasks did the AI handle? How long did each take? What was the error rate? Capture these numbers — they are your Week 1 benchmark.
  • 16:00–17:00 YOU Close the sprint. You have a working AI system. Document what you built: the workflow, the configuration, the test results. This documentation will let you replicate it or hand it off.
  • 17:00–18:00 YOU Post-mortem: What took longer than expected? What would you do differently? What is the next play or the next workflow to automate? The sprint is done. The system is live. Now you iterate.
After 72 hours: You have a live AI system. Run it for 2 weeks before changing anything. Collect data. Let it stabilize. Then optimize. The mistake is tweaking configurations weekly before the system has had time to show you what it actually does at scale.
